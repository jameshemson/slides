# Delivery

The skill builds decks, not speakers, so delivery is kept lean. This file does two
jobs: it shapes the speaker notes the build produces, and it gives the user a short
primer on presenting the finished deck. The skill's weight sits in Plan, Create, and
Build; delivery is the light strand.

## Presentation and communication are not the same thing

The presentation is everything behind the speaker: the slides. The communication is
everything in front: the speaker and the audience, in a room together. A deck that
only moves information is a document. The job is the communication. A speaker who
reads the slides has become a messenger to their own presentation.

## Body, voice, words

Communication is carried by the body, the voice, and the words, weighted in that
order. The words matter least of the three, which is why a deck cannot do the job
alone.

- **Body.** Stand relaxed, open, and grounded. Move with purpose, not nerves. Hold
  eye contact, gesture, and smile. Slow, economical movement reads as authority;
  restless movement reads as nerves.
- **Voice.** Work pitch, projection, pace, and pause, and speak clearly. The voice
  has a wide range and most speakers use a narrow slice of it. Use more of it. A
  flat voice loses the room whatever the slides do.
- **Words.** Signpost where the talk is going. Carry it with stories. Cut weak-speak,
  so "I think this might" becomes "I believe this will". Cut jargon.

## Think, breathe, speak

Under pressure the order slips and the speaker talks before thinking. Hold the
order: think, then breathe, then speak. The pause that order creates is not dead
air. Silence gives the audience time to absorb and gives the speaker time to land
the next point. It is fine to say nothing for a moment.

Nerves are adrenalin, and a presenter needs some of it. The job is not to remove the
nerves but to stay on top of them. Win the audience's trust before winning their
agreement; an audience reads insincerity quickly.

## The slide is not the script

The slide supports the speaker. It is never the speaker's lines. Text written to be
read aloud word for word fails both as a slide and as delivery. What the speaker
says lives in the speaker notes, not on the slide.

## Speaker notes

The build writes speaker notes for each slide. They carry what the presenter says,
or, for a read deck, the context a reader needs to make sense of the slide alone.
Notes are prose, so they are held to the prose-slop standard in `slop.md`: plain
words, active voice, varied sentence length, no filler. A note is a prompt for the
speaker, not a transcript to be recited.
