# Slides: visual craft

How the slides themselves are made, once the story and the outline are agreed. The
skill is brand-agnostic and never imposes a look. The craft below holds for every
brand; the user's own template supplies the skin.

## A slide is not a document

A slide stands behind the presenter. It reinforces what the presenter says; it does
not repeat it. The audience can listen or read, not both at once, so a slide built
to be read aloud word for word wastes the audience's attention and turns the
presenter into a narrator of their own slides.

This holds across registers, and the treatment flexes:

- A **presented** slide is close to a billboard. It carries a few words, a hero
  number, an image, or a framework, and leans on the speaker for the detail.
- A **read** or data-led slide has no narrator, so it carries more on its own. A
  chart beside three or four tight supporting points is the right treatment for a
  quarter's results, not a failure of craft.

What never changes is that a wall of words fails in either register. Text on a slide
earns its place by being sharp, not by being absent.

## One idea per slide

Slides are free. A crowded slide is two slides. Split anything that carries more
than one point.

One idea does not mean three words. A chart and the few lines that explain it are
one idea, because everything on the slide serves a single point. The test is whether
every element serves that point, not how much text sits on the slide. The slide
takes whatever form its one idea needs: a single image, a hero number, a chart with
its key points, a diagram of a concept or process, a two-part comparison.

## Hero the one thing

Every slide has one element that matters most: a number, a phrase, or an image. Size
it and place it so the audience cannot miss it. Everything else on the slide
recedes. A slide where everything competes for attention has no focus at all.

## Show with real images

Use full-bleed imagery of real things. A real image fills the slide; it is not a
small picture boxed into a corner. Avoid decorative clip-art and stock cliché. Crop
an image to its subject. Where text sits over an image, marry the two: use the empty
space in the frame, a blurred area, or the direction of a subject's gaze to seat the
words. One image per slide.

## Structure the audience can feel

Give the deck a spine the audience can sense. Use section dividers to mark each new
part, signpost as the deck moves, and keep one consistent system throughout, so the
audience always knows where they are and what is coming.

## Restraint

The look is built from deliberate choices, held with discipline:

- **Hierarchy.** Make the order of importance visible. The eye should land on the
  most important thing first.
- **Layout.** Place elements for how eyes move across a slide. The top-left is the
  hottest spot. Lead the eye along a clear path.
- **Alignment and grid.** Align everything to a grid. Misaligned elements jump and
  read as careless.
- **Whitespace.** Empty space is a tool. A slide with room to breathe carries more
  than a slide filled edge to edge.
- **Colour.** Keep the palette tight: a primary used often, a secondary used
  sparingly. Too much colour is a real fault. Some colours carry meaning, so red
  reads as danger or stop; use it on purpose.
- **Contrast.** Contrast in size, shape, shade, colour, or proximity tells the
  audience what relates to what and what matters most. Contrast must mean something.
  Accidental contrast misleads the audience and reads as a mistake.

## The user's brand carries the look

Fonts, colours, and layouts come from the user's own template, captured up front by
the teach-slides skill and recorded in the brand profile. The craft above is
constant across every brand. The skin is the user's. The skill never invents a look
or overrides the template.

## Build scenes, not isolated slides

Design the deck as a sequence, not a stack of separate slides. One useful method is
to start from the final slide, the one idea the audience must leave with, and work
backwards, so every slide before it sets that ending up.
