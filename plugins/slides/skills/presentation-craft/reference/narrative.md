# Narrative: Plan and Create

The thinking before the slides. Most of the work in making a presentation happens
here, in two stages. Plan settles who the deck is for and the job it has to do.
Create settles what it says and the shape it takes. No slide is designed until both
are done.

## Plan: who, why, how it will be used

### Know the register

Decide whether the deck will be presented live or read without a narrator. A board
pack, an earnings update, and a status report travel alone, so each slide carries
its own meaning. A presented deck leans on the speaker and stays spare. Most decks
sit between the two, and many are both: presented once, then circulated. The
register sets how hard each slide has to work, and it is fixed here, in Plan, not
discovered later. See `slides.md` for how the treatment flexes.

### Segment and understand the audience

Audience work is not demographics. Map their objectives, their knowledge level, their
values, and what they were doing before they walked in. Picture one real person who
fits the room and write for them. When several audiences share one room, understand
each one, address each one, and connect each back to the whole. A presentation
written for no one in particular reads as a letter addressed to nobody.

### Find the common ground

Name what the presenter and the audience already share. The deck builds out from
that common ground. Without it the audience has no reason to follow.

### Think, Feel, Do

Write three short statements: what the audience should **think** by the time they
leave (the core idea), what they should **feel** (the emotional shift), and what
they should **do** (the action). This is the brief for everything that follows. A
slide that serves none of the three does not belong in the deck.

### Move from, move to

State where the audience is now and where they need to be. The gap between those
two points is the presentation. Everything in the deck closes that gap.

## Create: the content

Generate the content, then give it a shape. Do this as a back-and-forth with the
user, spitballing freely, before any slide exists.

### The one idea

Write a single sentence: the point of view, and why the audience should care. This
is the unique angle the deck exists to land. Find it before anything else.
Everything in the deck serves it.

### Storyboard before slides

Work the content as a storyboard, in this order:

1. **Generate.** Put down every idea, freely, without filtering.
2. **Filter.** Keep only what serves the one idea. Cut the rest, however good it is.
   Filter for coverage with no overlaps and no gaps: overlapping points confuse,
   missing points leave a hole.
3. **Cluster.** Group the survivors into three to five messages.
4. **Order.** Sequence the messages for impact.
5. **Add support.** Attach the facts and evidence that back each message.

The storyboard is the deck's structure, agreed before a slide is designed. It is
shareable, it shows the whole at a glance, and working it with the user builds their
ownership of the result.

### More than facts

Faced with facts that contradict them, people dig in. A story lets the audience
reach the conclusion on their own and make it theirs. So story leads and facts
support. Pressure-test each message against four questions:

- **Story.** Is there a story carrying this, not a bare claim? Tell the story first,
  then the facts.
- **Emotion.** Does it make the audience feel something? Not every message has to
  move someone to tears, but a message that lands nowhere does not stick.
- **Concrete.** Is it concrete rather than abstract? Abstract ideas slide off.
  People who know a subject well forget how it sounds to those who do not, so name
  the real thing in plain, physical language.
- **Simple.** Is it reduced to its core? Find the single most important thing the
  message must carry and lead with it. Simple is prioritisation, not dumbing down.

Reach past the feature to the benefit, and past the benefit to what the benefit lets
the audience do. Speak to identity: not only what the audience gets, but who they
become.

### Earn the emotion

Emotion is essential and it is not slop. Unearned emotion is. Earn it through
something true and concrete: a real situation, a real consequence, a real person.
Never manufacture it with adjectives, hype, or sensational claims. The slop detector
in `slop.md` passes earned emotion and catches only the unearned kind.

## Shape and structure

### The story shape

Every presentation has a shape. The canonical spine has five beats:

1. **Situation and problem.** Set the scene and name what is wrong.
2. **Make it relatable.** Bring it close with an example, a detail, or a metaphor.
3. **The turn.** The moment of hope: it does not have to be this way.
4. **The audience's role.** What the audience does about it.
5. **One-sentence summary.** The single line they carry out of the room.

Underneath, a presentation still has a beginning, a middle, and an end: tell the
audience what is coming, tell them, then tell them what they heard. The classic
narrative arc also holds: a situation rises through tension to a peak, then settles
into a resolution.

### Order for impact

The same facts in a different order can demotivate or motivate. Order is a
deliberate choice, not the sequence the facts arrived in. Build flow by story, by
chronology, by step-by-step sequence, or by climax, climbing from least to most
important. Where there is a contrast to draw, use it: problem and solution, compare
and contrast, cause and effect, advantage and disadvantage.

### Open and close hard

Open with something that grabs the room and magnifies the one idea: a
demonstration, a repeatable line, a striking image, a true story, a stat that lands.
The opening is never decoration. Signpost the structure as the deck moves so the
audience always knows where they are. Close on a high, and bookend the open so the
audience leaves remembering the one idea.

The agreed story plus a slide-by-slide outline is the output of this stage. Only
then does slide design begin.
