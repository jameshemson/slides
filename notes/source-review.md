## The core method — one coherent system

Every deck James teaches is the same method, refined 2020 → 2024. The spine:

**PLAN → CREATE → DELIVER** — "the 3 key steps for presenting"
- **Plan** — why, where & who you are talking to
- **Create** — what you talk about
- **Deliver** — how you talk about it

`Powerful Presenting` is the complete version. The storytelling decks cover Plan + Create. `Bluey` is the shortest, freshest cut and carries James's most recent structural thinking. This spine is the natural backbone for the `narrative` skill.

---

## PLAN → `narrative.md`

- **You are not the hero.** The presenter is the *mentor*, not the hero — Dumbledore not Harry; Bandit & Chilli not Bluey. "Mentors are selfless and think of themselves in the context of others." The audience is the hero; you guide them. *"Designing a presentation without an audience in mind is like writing a love letter and addressing it 'To whom it may concern'"* (Ken Haemer). "You're giving the audience a gift."
- **Who — understand & segment the audience.** Basics: size, objectives/OKRs, why attending, name & role, knowledge level. Deeper: competitors, blogs/social, lifestyle, values, knowledge, motivations — "not just demographics." "Think of someone you know personally." Segment and address each sub-audience (Reagan's Challenger speech: mourners, families, schoolchildren, NASA, the USSR — each addressed, all connected to the whole).
- **Why — common ground + Think/Feel/Do.** Find and increase the common ground between presenter and audience. "Why is it taking place? Move from → move to." **Think / Feel / Do**: write the three words, brainstorm how the audience should change by the time they leave. Think = the core idea; Feel = the emotional connection; Do = the action/takeaway.
- **Where** — environment, timings, equipment. The practical stuff; lightest-weight strand, least relevant to a deck-building tool.

## CREATE → `narrative.md` + `slides.md`

- **The big idea / sticky idea.** A unique point of view, in one sentence, that says why the audience should care. Find it before anything else.
- **Storyboard before slides.** Collect ideas, then: **Generate → Filter → Cluster → Create messages → Order → Add support.** Filter with **MECE** (mutually exclusive — overlaps confuse; collectively exhaustive — don't leave gaps). "Kill your darlings": cut the post-its that don't serve the big idea. Storyboarding is shareable, builds buy-in (Ikea effect), shows the whole at a glance. **This is the "spitball before you touch slides" step the skill is built around — it is already James's method.**
- **More than just facts** — the heart of it. Don't list facts; appeal to head, heart and gut. The framework is the Heath brothers' **SUCCESs** (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories); James most often teaches the short form **Story / Emotion / Concrete / Simple**:
  - **Story** — stories are how the brain works. The Stanford capital-punishment study (Mercier & Sperber): facts alone harden existing views. Annette Simmons: *"Facts don't inspire. They don't make people believe."* A story lets the audience reach your conclusion themselves — "make your story their story." **Tell a story first, then facts.** Hemingway's six-word story. Wall-E's wordless first 40 minutes.
  - **Emotion** — "not every story has to make someone cry, but it has to make someone feel." Earned through a true, concrete example (Blue Planet II's pilot whale; 88% changed behaviour). Benefits over features, and the **benefit of the benefit** (drill → hole → hang your children's pictures). Appeal to identity: "not what's in it for me, but who am I, what do people like me do here."
  - **Concrete** — abstract ideas don't stick. The **Curse of Knowledge** (the Tap-Tap-Tap tappers/listeners exercise; Elizabeth Newton). Beat it by making ideas concrete: JFK's "put a man on the moon and return him safely," not "lunar-based next-generation functionalities."
  - **Simple** — find the *core* ("commander's intent"). Southwest's "we are THE low-cost airline." Not dumbing down — "elegance and prioritisation."
  - Worked exemplars that embody all of it: **TOMS** one-for-one, **Patagonia** "Don't Buy This Jacket."
- **Bluey's refined story structure (2024, James's latest):** ❓ Situation & problem → 💗 Make it relatable (example, detail, metaphor) → 💡 Solution & hope (the "but it doesn't have to be that way" turning point) → 🍴 Audience's role → ✅ Single-sentence summary. Plus **Freytag's Pyramid** (situation, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution) and "find the common humanity — specific *and* speaks to everyone."

## SHAPE & STRUCTURE → `narrative.md`

- **Tell them what you're going to tell them / tell them / tell them what you've told them** = Beginning / Middle / End = Intro & Objectives / Content / Summary.
- **Is there a flow?** Storylike, chronological, sequential (step-by-step), climactic (least → most important).
- **Can you contrast?** Problem–solution, compare–contrast, cause–effect, advantage–disadvantage.
- **Order for impact.** Same facts, different order: the "revenue is down" example reordered from demotivating to motivating. End on a high.
- **Start with a BANG** — a real attention grabber (dramatised demo, repeatable soundbite, evocative visual, emotive story, genuinely shocking stat) that *magnifies* the sticky idea, never decoration. Bookend with a second bang / "remember me." Signpost throughout.
- Duarte's **sparkline** (alternate "what is" with "what could be" to create tension) and audience-journey structures (the Explanation, the Pitch, the Drama, Situation–Complication–Resolution) — from the Visme guide, consistent with James's method.

## DELIVER → `delivery.md`

From `Powerful Presenting` Day 2, the Vanquis deck, and the RADA manual. This strand is about *live presenting*, so for a deck-building skill it likely informs the **speaker notes** the build generates plus a short delivery primer — see open question 5.

- **Music / Dance / Lyrics** (the Mehrabian split): voice 38%, body 55%, words 7%.
  - **Music (voice):** Pitch, Projection, Pace, Pause, Clarity. The voice can do 24 notes; most use 3; aim for 8.
  - **Dance (body):** relaxed open grounded posture, purposeful movement, eye contact, gesture, smile. "Slow movement is majestic"; economy of movement reads as authority (Borg vs Connors).
  - **Lyrics (words):** signpost, anecdotes, cut weak-speak ("I think/may/could" → "I believe/know"), cut jargon.
- **RADA: Mind / Body / Breath / Voice** and the formula **Think → Breathe → Speak.** Pause; "it's OK to say nothing." Nerves are adrenalin — necessary, just don't drown. "Win hearts then minds — we always recognise insincerity."
- **Presentation vs communication** — "presentation is everything behind you (the slides); communication is everything in front (you and the audience)." Don't be a messenger to your own slides.

## VISUAL CRAFT → `slides.md`

- **Slides are a billboard, not a document.** The audience can listen or read, not both — so the slide *reinforces* your words, never repeats them. The slide is the image; you are the voice.
- **One idea per slide.** "Slides are free" — split a busy slide.
- James's signature teaching device: the **Third Silesian War** demo — a dense Wikipedia paragraph progressively rebuilt (wall of text → hierarchy → Q&A → key facts → one image). And the Lorem-Ipsum slide: *"No one will read this in a presentation… and you'll just end up reading it out."*
- **Design elements:** contrast (size/shape/shade/colour/proximity — "just noticeable difference", Tufte; unintended contrast confuses); hierarchy; layout for human eyes (F/Z-pattern, hot top-left); grid & alignment (misalignment makes things "jump"); colour discipline (theme colours, primary often / secondary sparingly, "too much colour" is real, red = danger, 60-30-10).
- **Whitespace** — more powerful than filling the slide.
- **Images:** avoid clichéd stock (hands over a globe); choose natural; full-bleed beats small; one image per slide; crop to the subject; marry text to image via empty space, blur or gaze direction.
- **Stick to the brand guidelines** — James already teaches building within a brand's colours, type and template. This is the hook for the skill's brand-agnostic design: `teach-slides` captures the brand, `build-deck` works within it.
- **Builds:** start from the final slide and work backwards; "create scenes, not isolated slides" (Duarte).

## DATA & CHARTS → `data-viz.md`

- Find your **key message first**, then choose the chart. Make the key point stand out (colour the insight, grey the rest); push non-essential elements back; 2–3 colours max; drop gridlines/borders; label directly, kill legends.
- **The Graphic Continuum** (Schwabish & Ribecca) — chart families by the relationship being shown: Distribution, Time, Comparing Categories, Geospatial, Part-to-Whole, Relationship. Encode the *taxonomy* with attribution.
- The two `Data Viz/` chart-choosers reinforce "pick the chart from the question." Visual pass pending.

## ANTI-PATTERNS → `slop.md`

James's own decks are the **positive reference** (what good looks like); Claude's habits are what the detector must **catch**. Anti-patterns James explicitly teaches against:

- **The wall of text** — the slide as a document / teleprompter; "you'll just end up reading it out."
- **Facts without story** — a deck that is only data. "Facts don't inspire."
- **Being the hero** — a deck about the presenter or product, not the audience.
- **Abstract over concrete** — "team-centred innovation, lunar-based next-generation functionalities."
- **Corporate waffle & weak-speak** — the RADA before/after ("robust quality control cascaded through the organisation…" → "we are committed to good service").
- **Unintended contrast; too much colour; clichéd stock photos; gradient-and-curve template backgrounds; purposeless animation.**
- **Leftover template boilerplate** — observed live in `Storytelling copy.pptx` (slides 24–37: "This is a Divider Slide", "COLUMN 1 COLUMN 2", "EMILY MAGINESS / HEAD OF MARKETING", placeholder lorem). Leftover scaffolding is a real slop tell.

**The crucial distinction for the detector.** James teaches emotion, persuasion, "make them feel", benefit-of-the-benefit, "what could be" — *hard*. That is **not** slop. The slop is **unearned** emotion: sensationalising, ad-copy hype, adjective soup with nothing concrete beneath it. James's emotion is always **earned** — a true, specific, concrete story (a real whale, a real pair of TOMS, a real hole in his trouser pocket on a first date). The detector must pass earned emotion and catch the unearned kind. It must not simply flag "emotional language."

Note: James's decks do **not** show the title → body → strapline pattern the user flagged as Claude's habit. His slides are a few words, or a framework diagram, or image-only, or a single quote. The strapline anti-pattern is a Claude tic; James's decks are the counter-example.

## Decisions (James, 2026-05-16)

1. **Spine confirmed** — Plan → Create → Deliver is the backbone of the `narrative` skill.
2. **Bluey leads** — Bluey's 2024 story structure (Situation/Problem → Relatable → Solution/Hope → Audience's role → One-sentence summary, plus Freytag) is the canonical spine. `Powerful Presenting` and the Storytelling decks supply the depth and worked detail beneath it.
3. **Principle-only craft references** — strip all named examples. Wall-E, Bandit/Dumbledore, Blue Planet, TOMS, Patagonia, JFK, Southwest, the Third Silesian War, the hole-in-the-trousers story: none appear in the skill's reference files, and none in generated decks. The reference files teach the *principle*; every deck the skill builds uses the user's own material. (This review document still names them — it is a record of the source, not skill output.)
4. **Emotion line confirmed** — the slop detector passes *earned* emotion (a true, concrete story) and catches only *unearned* emotion (sensationalism, ad-copy hype).
5. **Delivery is lean** — `delivery.md` mainly shapes the speaker notes the build generates, plus a short primer. The skill's centre of gravity is Plan + Create + Build.
6. **Register matters (added by James)** — the skill handles both presented decks (spare, image-led) and read or data-led decks (earnings, board packs, status updates), where a chart with three or four tight supporting points is the right treatment. "One idea per slide" holds in both; the treatment flexes by register. The slop detector judges text *quality*, not text *presence*.
7. **Concept diagrams are first-class (added by James)** — a diagram that explains a process, structure, relationship or journey is a distinct treatment from a data chart, and a slide may legitimately pair text with a strong visual or infographic. Diagrams must simplify, not decorate. (`Thinking/Communicating Visually` is the source deck for this.)
8. **Prose-level slop adopts stop-slop (added by James)** — the slop detector gains a second layer from `stop-slop` (Hardik Pandya, github.com/hardikpandya/stop-slop, MIT) for sentence-and-word AI tells: banned phrases, banned structures, quick checks, a five-dimension score. Applied in full to speaker notes, in spirit to terse slide copy. Full spec in `slop-detector.md`.

## Visual pass — done

Three decks rendered and reviewed: `Bluey`, `20210608 Storytelling`, `Thinking/Communicating Visually`. The decisive finding: **the same craft method, rendered in three completely different visual skins** — Bluey's playful blue and IP; the 2021 workshop's AND Digital corporate red/black/white; the visual-thinking deck's hand-drawn black-and-white sketchnotes. The craft is constant; the brand skin is fully swappable. This is the strongest validation of the brand-agnostic design.

Consistent visual patterns across all three:
- Image-led, one idea per slide, held strictly.
- Hero treatment of the single key element (a number, a phrase, an image).
- A consistent section-divider system (one short word on a brand colour field).
- Full-bleed photography for real-world examples, never boxed-in thumbnails.
- Plan/Create/Deliver drawn as a clean numbered diagram.
- Yellow sticky notes for the storyboard process, often shown building across slides.
- Bold typographic treatment for key statements and quotes.
- A restrained, brand-driven palette.
- No straplines, no bullet soup. The recurring exception is a dense "ways of working" housekeeping slide — a workshop artifact, not a content slide.

These feed `slides.md` and give the slop detector its positive reference for what good looks like.

The distilled, principle-only method is written up separately in `approach.md`.
