# The slides approach

How the `slides` skill thinks about making a presentation. This distils a body of teaching material (recorded in `source-review.md`) into one method. It is principle-only by design: no worked examples live here or in the skill, and every deck the skill builds uses the user's own material.

## Premise

Three ideas shape everything below.

**A presentation is communication, not information transfer.** The slides are what stands behind you. The communication is what happens in front: you and the audience. A deck that only moves information is a document. The job is to change what the audience thinks, feels, and does.

**You are not the hero.** The presenter is the mentor. The audience is the hero, and the mentor's job is to get them somewhere. A deck that is about the presenter, or about how good the product is, has the roles reversed.

**Story moves people; facts alone do not.** Faced with facts that contradict them, people dig in. A story lets the audience reach the conclusion themselves and make it their own. So story comes first, with facts in support. A pile of facts is not persuasion.

## The method: Plan, Create, Deliver

This is the arc of making any presentation. The skill spends most of its effort in Plan and Create, the thinking, before a single slide exists.

### Plan: who, why, and how it will be used

Before content, understand the room and the job the deck has to do.

- **Know the register.** Is this a deck you will present live, or one that is read without you, such as a board pack, an earnings update, or a status report? A presented deck leans on you and stays spare. A read or data-led deck has no narrator, so each slide has to carry its own meaning. Most decks sit between the two, and many are both: presented once, then circulated. The register sets how hard each slide has to work, and it is decided here, in Plan.
- **Segment and understand the audience.** Not demographics. Their objectives, knowledge, values, and motivations, and what they were doing before they walked in. Where several audiences share one room, each is understood and addressed, then connected to the whole.
- **Think, Feel, Do.** Name, in three short statements, what the audience should think (the core idea), feel (the emotional shift), and do (the action) by the time they leave. This is the brief for everything that follows. A slide that serves none of the three does not belong.
- **Move from, move to.** State where the audience is now and where they need to be. The gap between the two is the presentation.

### Create: what

Content, then shape. The skill does this as a back-and-forth with the user, spitballing, before it builds anything.

- **The one idea.** A single sentence: the point of view, and why the audience should care. Everything in the deck serves it.
- **Storyboard before slides.** Generate ideas freely. Filter to only what serves the one idea, and cut the rest however much you like it. Cluster into three to five messages. Order them. The storyboard is the deck's structure, settled before any slide is designed.
- **More than facts.** Each message is pressure-tested: is there a story; is it concrete rather than abstract; does it carry emotion; is it simple, reduced to its core. Facts inform. These four make it land and stick.
- **The story shape.** Every presentation has a shape: situation and problem, then make it relatable, then the turn (it does not have to be this way), then the audience's role, then a single-sentence takeaway. Within that, order for impact. The same facts in a different order can demotivate or motivate; the order is a deliberate choice.
- **Earn the emotion.** Emotion is essential, and it is not slop. Unearned emotion is. Emotion is earned through something true and concrete: a real situation, a real consequence, a real person. It is never manufactured with adjectives, hype, or sensational claims.

### Deliver: how (kept lean)

The skill builds decks, not speakers, so delivery is light. It shapes the speaker notes the build produces and offers a short primer. The primer's substance: communication is body, voice, and words, weighted in that order; think, then breathe, then speak; pause; the slide supports you and is not your script.

## Visual craft

How the slides themselves are made. The skill is brand-agnostic and never imposes a look, but the craft below holds for every brand.

- **Match the slide to the register.** A presented slide is close to a billboard: it reinforces what you say rather than repeating it, and leans on you for the detail. A read or data-led slide carries more on its own; a chart beside three or four tight supporting points is the right treatment for a quarter's results, not a failure of craft. The slide takes the form its one idea needs: a single image, a hero number, a chart with its key points, a diagram that makes a concept or process clear, a two-part comparison. What never changes across registers is that a slide is not a script; text to be read aloud word for word, or a wall of words, fails either way.
- **One idea per slide.** Slides are free; a crowded slide is two slides. But one idea does not mean three words: a chart and the few points that explain it are still one idea. The test is whether everything on the slide serves a single point, not how much text is on it.
- **Hero the one thing.** The single most important element, a number or a phrase or an image, is sized and placed like it matters. Everything else recedes.
- **Show, with real images.** Full-bleed imagery for real things. Not small images boxed into a corner, not decorative clip-art, not stock cliché.
- **Structure you can feel.** Section dividers, signposting, a consistent system, so the audience always knows where they are.
- **Restraint.** Deliberate hierarchy, alignment, whitespace, and a tight palette. Contrast is used to mean something; accidental contrast misleads.
- **The user's brand carries the look.** Fonts, colours, and layouts come from the user's own template, captured up front. The craft is constant. The skin is theirs.

## Data, charts and diagrams

- **Message first, chart second.** Decide what the data needs to say, then choose the chart that says it.
- **Choose the chart by relationship.** Pick the chart from the relationship being shown: comparison, change over time, part-to-whole, distribution, correlation, geography.
- **Make the point stand out.** Colour the insight, mute everything else. Drop gridlines and borders. Label directly instead of using legends.
- **Use a diagram to make a concept simple.** When an idea is a process, a structure, a relationship or a journey, a diagram carries it better than a paragraph or a list: a flow, a cycle, a hierarchy, a set of steps, a layered model, a before-and-after. The diagram's shape mirrors the idea's shape, holds one concept, and gives the eye a single clear path to follow.
- **A diagram must simplify, not decorate.** If it adds complexity, uses boxes and arrows for their own sake, or needs a key to decode, it has failed. A diagram that obscures is slop; one that makes the idea click is craft.

## The slop line

The skill actively detects and refuses presentation slop. The full taxonomy, including a prose-level layer adopted from `stop-slop` (Hardik Pandya, MIT), is specified in `slop-detector.md` and becomes the skill's `slop` reference. The line itself:

- **Earned over unearned emotion.** Emotion grounded in something true and concrete is kept. Sensationalism, ad-copy voice, and hype with nothing concrete beneath it are refused.
- **Bad text, not all text.** Walls of text, bullet soup (many loose, sentence-long, unrelated points), and a slide written as a script to be read aloud are refused. A chart with a few tight, parallel supporting points is not slop; it is the right treatment for a data slide. The test is whether the text earns its place and is sharp, not whether text exists.
- **Decoration is not communication.** A diagram, chart, image or animation only earns its place if the idea is clearer with it than without it. Boxes and arrows for their own sake, a diagram that needs a key to decode, chartjunk, an image chosen to fill space: all removed. A visual must make the idea click, not show effort.
- **No tacked-on straplines.** A title, then the content, then a tagline floated at the bottom of the slide is an AI tic, not a design. It is refused by name.
- **Say the real thing.** Corporate waffle, weak-speak, and abstraction are replaced with plain, concrete language.
- **No leftover scaffolding.** Placeholder text, unedited template furniture, and lorem ipsum never ship.

## How the skill applies this

Four skills carry the method end to end:

1. **teach-slides** captures the user's brand once: template, fonts, colours, voice, audience norms. Everything afterwards is built in that brand.
2. **narrative** is the spitball. It works through Plan and Create with the user, pushes back, refuses to jump to slides, and ends with an agreed story and a slide-by-slide outline.
3. **build-deck** turns that outline into a real PowerPoint file, filling the user's own template, and runs the slop detector before it delivers.
4. **slop-check** is the detector on its own, run against any deck or outline.

The build plan for these four skills is in `PLAN.md` at the repo root.
